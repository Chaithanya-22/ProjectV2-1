Mercury

Overview of Mercury

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and the smallest in the solar system, with a diameter of about 4,880 kilometers. It orbits the Sun at an average distance of approximately 57.9 million kilometers and completes one revolution in just 88 Earth days. Due to its proximity to the Sun, Mercury experiences extreme temperature fluctuations, ranging from scorching highs of about 430°C (800°F) during the day to freezing lows of -180°C (-290°F) at night.

Surface and Composition

The surface of Mercury is heavily cratered, resembling that of the Moon, and features large cliffs known as "lobate scarps." These cliffs are thought to be the result of the planet's contraction as it cooled. Mercury's composition is primarily metallic, with a large iron core that makes up about 75% of its volume. The thin atmosphere, composed mostly of oxygen, sodium, hydrogen, and helium, is insufficient to retain heat, contributing to the planet’s extreme temperature variations.

Exploration and Mysteries

Mercury has been visited by two spacecraft: Mariner 10 and MESSENGER. MESSENGER provided invaluable data about the planet’s geology and magnetic field. Despite these explorations, many mysteries remain, such as the nature of its core and the existence of ice in permanently shadowed craters. Understanding Mercury is crucial for comprehending the formation and evolution of the inner solar system.
